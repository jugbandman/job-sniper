# Andy's Cover Letter Style Guide

**Date Created**: November 6, 2025
**Source**: SugarCRM Channel Sales Director application

---

## Writing Style Preferences

### Tone & Voice
- ✅ **Conversational and authentic** - Write like Andy talks, not like a corporate sales pitch
- ✅ **Confident but humble** - "helped the partner reps with handling" vs "I managed"
- ✅ **Personal touches welcome** - References to employees, location preferences, real company names
- ✅ **Less formal** - "I'm interested in" vs "I am pleased to submit my application for"
- ❌ **Avoid over-polished/sales-y** - Don't sound like a marketing brochure

### Structure
- ✅ **Shorter, punchier paragraphs** - Get to the point quickly
- ✅ **Less structured formatting** - Don't overuse bold headers and bullets
- ✅ **Natural flow** - Conversational progression, not rigid sections
- ❌ **Avoid overly structured** - Not every section needs a bold header

### Opening
**Andy's Style**:
> "I'm applying for the Channel Sales Director role because I'm interested in leveraging my enterprise sales, team-building, and direct channel/partner management experience to helping scale your 230+ partner ecosystem."

**What Works**:
- Gets straight to the point
- "I'm interested in" (conversational) not "I am writing to express my interest in"
- Immediately mentions relevant experience
- Natural, direct voice

### Career Context
**Andy's Style**:
> "My last 4-5 years have been with early stage underdogs, the next step in my career will be applying my learnings with a strong market leader."

**What Works**:
- Personal career narrative
- Honest about context ("early stage underdogs" vs trying to make it sound fancy)
- Shows intentionality about next move
- Conversational language

### Specific Details
**Andy's Style**:
> "At Resolve, I sold to MSPs, primarily Cognizant."

**What Works**:
- Real company names (not generic "Fortune 500 clients")
- Specific and authentic
- Builds credibility through details

### Personality & Authenticity
**Andy's Style**:
> "I'm genuinely excited about this role and company, your employees have said great things. Also a Denver office is welcome after 5 years of full remote."

**What Works**:
- Personal touch (talked to employees)
- Honest about preferences (wants Denver office after remote)
- "Also..." feels conversational, not scripted
- Shows he's done homework (talked to employees)

### Length
- ✅ **Shorter than my drafts** - Andy's version is more concise
- ✅ **Don't over-explain** - Trust the reader to connect dots
- ❌ **Avoid verbose explanations** - Cut unnecessary words

## What to Avoid (Based on My Original Draft)

### ❌ Too Formal
**My Draft**:
> "Dear James and the SugarCRM Hiring Team,"

**Andy's Version**:
> "Dear Hiring Team,"

**Lesson**: Keep it simple. Don't assume who's reading.

### ❌ Too Sales-y
**My Draft**:
> "You might be wondering: 'Andy's never been a Channel Sales Director - why should we hire him?' Fair question. Here's why I'm uniquely qualified..."

**Andy's Version**: (Doesn't pre-emptively defend gaps - just leads with strengths)

**Lesson**: Don't sell too hard. Trust your experience to speak for itself.

### ❌ Over-Structured
**My Draft**: Used lots of bold headers, structured sections (What I Bring:, Why SugarCRM:, etc.)

**Andy's Version**: More natural flow, less rigid structure

**Lesson**: Don't make it feel like a template. Make it feel like a thoughtful letter.

### ❌ Too Perfect
**My Draft**: Very polished, no typos, perfect grammar

**Andy's Version**: Has a small typo ("ull remote" instead of "full remote"), feels more authentic

**Lesson**: Don't over-edit. Authentic > perfect.

## Andy's Cover Letter Formula

### Opening Paragraph:
1. State the role
2. Briefly mention relevant experience (channel, enterprise, team-building)
3. Add personal context if relevant (career stage, why this move)
4. Show understanding of both sides (managing partners + selling to partners)

### Why This Company:
1. Acknowledge their success (specific metrics)
2. Connect role focus to your experience
3. Personal touch (what you've learned about them, why you're excited)

### What You Bring (3-4 Key Strengths):
1. **Enterprise Sales**: Specific companies (Boeing, Intel), specific results (110%+ quota)
2. **Technical Credibility**: Former role (Engineering Manager), how it helps (enable partners)
3. **Team Building**: Three examples with specific outcomes (Resolve, Swarmia, Scaled Agile)
4. **Growth Operator**: Metrics (20x, $2M pipeline, 100% NRR)

### Channel/Partner Experience:
1. Scaled Agile context (what you learned, specific partners)
2. Resolve context (MSPs, specific insights)
3. Frame as "both sides" (managing + selling to)

### Closing:
1. Express genuine excitement
2. Personal touch (talked to employees, location preference, etc.)
3. Simple sign-off
4. Contact info

- "I understand X from both sides..."

## Length Guidelines

**Andy's SugarCRM Cover Letter**: ~650 words
**My Original Draft**: ~850 words

**Target**: 600-700 words
- Long enough to show depth
- Short enough to hold attention
- Hiring managers won't read 2 pages

---

## Formatting Preferences

### Minimal Structure:
- Brief opening paragraph
- "Why [Company]?" section
- "What I Bring:" with 3-4 strengths (each as a paragraph, not bullets)
- "My Partner/Channel Experience:" (if relevant)
- Brief closing

### Use Bold Sparingly:
- Section headers only (Why SugarCRM?, What I Bring:)
- Maybe 1-2 company names if they're impressive (Boeing, Intel)
- Don't bold every metric or achievement

### Paragraph Style:
- Mix of short (2-3 sentences) and medium (5-6 sentences) paragraphs
- Break up dense sections with white space
- Don't use bullet points unless listing items in a natural flow
