# Cover Letter Style Guide

A reference for how the agents should write cover letters. Based on the principle that authenticity beats polish.

---

## Writing Style Preferences

### Tone & Voice
- **Conversational and authentic** - Write like the candidate talks, not like a corporate pitch
- **Confident but humble** - "helped the team with" vs "I single-handedly managed"
- **Personal touches welcome** - References to employees, location preferences, real company names
- **Less formal** - "I'm interested in" vs "I am pleased to submit my application for"
- **Avoid over-polished/sales-y** - Don't sound like a marketing brochure

### Structure
- **Shorter, punchier paragraphs** - Get to the point quickly
- **Less rigid formatting** - Don't overuse bold headers and bullets
- **Natural flow** - Conversational progression, not rigid sections
- **Avoid over-structured** - Not every section needs a bold header

### Opening
> "I'm applying for [ROLE] because I'm interested in leveraging my [RELEVANT EXPERIENCE] to help [WHAT THEY NEED]."

**What Works:**
- Gets straight to the point
- "I'm interested in" (conversational) not "I am writing to express my interest in"
- Immediately mentions relevant experience

### Career Context
> "My last few years have been with early stage companies. The next step in my career is applying those learnings with a strong market leader."

**What Works:**
- Personal career narrative
- Honest about context (don't dress it up)
- Shows intentionality about next move

### Specific Details
> "At [Company], I sold to [Specific Customer Names]."

**What Works:**
- Real company names, not generic "Fortune 500 clients"
- Builds credibility through specifics

### Personality & Authenticity
> "I'm genuinely excited about this role. Your employees have said great things. Also a [city] office is welcome after years of full remote."

**What Works:**
- Personal touch (talked to employees)
- Honest about preferences
- Feels conversational, not scripted

---

## What to Avoid

### Too Formal
**Bad:** "Dear James and the Hiring Team,"
**Better:** "Dear Hiring Team,"
Keep it simple. Don't assume who's reading.

### Too Sales-y
**Bad:** "You might be wondering why you should hire me. Fair question. Here's why I'm uniquely qualified..."
**Better:** Don't pre-emptively defend gaps. Just lead with strengths.

### Over-Structured
**Bad:** Lots of bold headers, rigid sections (What I Bring:, Why Company:, etc.)
**Better:** Natural flow, like a thoughtful letter

### Too Perfect
**Bad:** Very polished, no personality
**Better:** Authentic voice beats perfect grammar

## Cover Letter Formula

### Opening Paragraph:
1. State the role
2. Briefly mention relevant experience
3. Add personal context if relevant (career stage, why this move)
4. Show understanding of both sides of the role

### Why This Company:
1. Acknowledge their success (specific metrics if possible)
2. Connect role focus to your experience
3. Personal touch (what you've learned about them)

### What You Bring (3-4 Key Strengths):
Each as a short paragraph, not bullets. Include specific companies, real metrics, concrete outcomes.

### Closing:
1. Express genuine excitement
2. Personal note (location, culture, what excites you)
3. Simple sign-off
4. Contact info

---

## Length Guidelines

**Target:** 600-700 words
- Long enough to show depth
- Short enough to hold attention
- Hiring managers won't read 2 pages

---

## Formatting Preferences

### Minimal Structure:
- Brief opening paragraph
- "Why [Company]?" section
- "What I Bring:" with 3-4 strengths (each as a paragraph, not bullets)
- Optional role-specific section
- Brief closing

### Use Bold Sparingly:
- Section headers only
- Maybe 1-2 company names if they're impressive
- Don't bold every metric

### Paragraph Style:
- Mix of short (2-3 sentences) and medium (5-6 sentences) paragraphs
- Break up dense sections with white space
- Don't use bullet points unless listing items in a natural flow

## For AI Agent: Key Instructions

When writing cover letters:

1. **Use conversational tone** - Write like the candidate talks
2. **Be specific** - Use real company names and real metrics from their profile
3. **Add personal touches** - Career context, why this move, what they've learned about the company
4. **Show both sides** - Frame experience from multiple perspectives
5. **Keep it concise** - 600-700 words
6. **Don't over-structure** - Natural flow over rigid sections
7. **Humble but confident** - "helped with" not "single-handedly drove"
8. **End with personality** - Personal note about what excites them
9. **Don't pre-emptively defend gaps** - Lead with strengths
10. **Trust the reader** - Don't over-explain
